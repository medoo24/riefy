Cellular Electrophysiology Cardiac electrical activity is governed by two distinct types of action potentials based on the presence of specific ion channels.
Myocardial Action Potential Phases (Fast-Response) Occurring in atrial and ventricular myocytes as well as Purkinje fibers, these cells utilize fast-response action potentials.
Phase 0 (Rapid Depolarization): Fast voltage-gated sodium (Na+) channels open, allowing a rapid inward Na+ current. The steepness of this phase determines the conduction velocity in these tissues.
Phase 1 (Initial Repolarization): Voltage-gated Na+ channels inactivate, and transient outward potassium (K+) channels open, causing a brief early fall in membrane voltage.
Phase 2 (Plateau): This phase is unique to cardiac muscle. Calcium (Ca2+) enters the cell through L-type voltage-gated Ca2+ channels, balancing the K+ efflux. This Ca2+ influx triggers further Ca2+ release from the sarcoplasmic reticulum (calcium-induced calcium release), initiating mechanical contraction.
Phase 3 (Rapid Repolarization): Ca2+ channels close while delayed-rectifier K+ channels open, leading to massive K+ efflux that returns the membrane toward its resting potential.
Phase 4 (Resting Potential): High K+ permeability maintains a stable, highly negative resting membrane potential until the next impulse.
Pacemaker Action Potential Phases (Slow-Response) Occurring in the sinoatrial (SA) and atrioventricular (AV) nodes, these cells lack a stable resting membrane potential and rely on slow Ca2+ currents.
Phase 4 (Spontaneous Diastolic Depolarization): This phase accounts for the automaticity of pacemaker cells. The "funny current" (\(I_f\)), a mixed inward Na+ and outward K+ current, gradually depolarizes the cell toward threshold. The slope of this phase dictates the heart rate and is modified by autonomic tone (sympathetic stimulation steepens it, parasympathetic flattens it).
Phase 0 (Upstroke): Once the threshold is reached, L-type Ca2+ channels open. Fast Na+ channels are permanently inactivated here, making this depolarization slower than in ventricular myocytes. This purposeful delay allows the AV node to prolong transmission, giving the ventricles time to fill.
Phases 1 and 2 are absent in pacemaker cells.
Phase 3 (Repolarization): Ca2+ channels inactivate and K+ channels open, returning the membrane toward its most negative potential.

Arrhythmogenic Mechanisms Arrhythmias arise from abnormalities in impulse formation, conduction, or a combination of both.
Abnormal Automaticity: Normally quiescent cells (such as working atrial or ventricular myocytes) develop spontaneous phase 4 depolarization. This is often provoked by ischemia, metabolic disturbances, electrolyte imbalances, or catecholamine excess. These arrhythmias typically accelerate and decelerate gradually. Examples include accelerated idioventricular rhythm and multifocal atrial tachycardia.
Triggered Activity: This mechanism requires a preceding action potential and is driven by membrane potential oscillations known as afterdepolarizations.
Early afterdepolarizations (EADs) occur during phase 2 or 3 of the action potential. They are favored by bradycardia, hypokalemia, and QT-prolonging drugs, and are the mechanism behind Torsades de pointes.
Delayed afterdepolarizations (DADs) occur during phase 4 and are usually the result of intracellular calcium overload, seen in conditions like digoxin toxicity or catecholamine excess.
Re-entry: This is the most common cause of tachyarrhythmias. It occurs when an impulse continuously circulates through a pathway, re-exciting myocardium after it has recovered. It requires three conditions: (1) a potential circuit, (2) unidirectional conduction block in part of the pathway, and (3) conduction slow enough for previously refractory tissue to recover before the impulse returns. Re-entry explains arrhythmias that start and stop abruptly, such as AVNRT, AVRT, atrial flutter, and scar-related monomorphic VT.
Conduction Failure: An impulse is delayed or completely blocked, resulting in bradyarrhythmias, AV blocks (like Mobitz I or II), bundle-branch blocks, or sinus exit blocks.

ECG Interpretation by Mechanism The surface ECG helps narrow the differential diagnosis based on QRS width (reflecting the route of ventricular activation) and rhythm regularity.
Narrow-Complex Tachycardias (QRS \(\le\) 120 ms) A narrow complex indicates that the ventricles are being rapidly activated via the normal His-Purkinje system, meaning the arrhythmia almost certainly originates from above the ventricles (supraventricular).
Regular: Sinus tachycardia, AV-nodal re-entrant tachycardia (AVNRT), orthodromic AV-re-entrant tachycardia (AVRT), focal atrial tachycardia, and atrial flutter with a fixed conduction block (e.g., 2:1 block).
Irregular: Atrial fibrillation, atrial flutter with variable AV block, and multifocal atrial tachycardia.
Wide-Complex Tachycardias (QRS > 120 ms) A wide complex indicates delayed or abnormal ventricular activation outside the normal His-Purkinje network. A critical safety rule dictates that any regular wide-complex tachycardia in an adult with structural heart disease must be treated as ventricular tachycardia (VT) until proven otherwise. Misclassifying VT as a supraventricular rhythm and administering AV-nodal blockers can cause severe hemodynamic deterioration.
Regular: Ventricular tachycardia (VT), supraventricular tachycardia (SVT) with aberrancy (bundle-branch block), antidromic AVRT, or a paced ventricular rhythm.
Irregular: Atrial fibrillation with bundle-branch block, polymorphic VT (including Torsades de pointes), or pre-excited atrial fibrillation (e.g., WPW syndrome). Note: Pre-excited AFib is an emergency; AV-nodal blocking drugs are contraindicated as they can drive all impulses down the accessory pathway, deteriorating into ventricular fibrillation.

Pharmacological Targets: Antiarrhythmic Classes The Vaughan Williams classification divides antiarrhythmics based on their primary electrophysiological action.
Class I (Sodium-Channel Blockers): These drugs slow phase 0 depolarization, with varying effects depending on the subclass.
Class IA (Quinidine, Procainamide, Disopyramide): Provide moderate Na+ blockade, prolong the action potential duration, increase the effective refractory period (ERP), and prolong the QT interval.
Class IB (Lidocaine, Phenytoin, Mexiletine): Provide weak Na+ blockade and uniquely decrease the action potential duration. They preferentially target ischemic or depolarized Purkinje and ventricular tissue, making them best for acute post-MI ventricular arrhythmias.
Class IC (Flecainide, Propafenone): Provide strong Na+ blockade, markedly prolonging the ERP in the AV node without affecting action potential duration. They are strictly contraindicated in patients with structural or ischemic heart disease due to severe proarrhythmic risks.
Class II (Beta-Blockers): Agents like metoprolol, propranolol, and esmolol reduce sympathetic tone. They decrease automaticity by reducing the slope of phase 4 spontaneous depolarization and heavily suppress AV-nodal conduction.
Class III (Potassium-Channel Blockers): Drugs such as amiodarone, sotalol, dofetilide, and ibutilide inhibit repolarization (Phase 3). This prolongs the action potential duration, the ERP, and the QT interval. Because of QT prolongation, sotalol and ibutilide carry a high risk of provoking Torsades de pointes. Amiodarone has mixed effects across all four classes and lower Torsades risk, but it carries widespread systemic toxicities (pulmonary, thyroid, hepatic).
Class IV (Calcium-Channel Blockers): Non-dihydropyridines like verapamil and diltiazem target L-type Ca2+ channels, significantly decreasing conduction velocity and increasing the ERP and PR interval, primarily affecting the AV node.

Cardiac Arrest Pathways Cardiac arrest management is strictly dichotomized by the initial rhythm into shockable and non-shockable pathways.
Shockable Rhythms: Ventricular Fibrillation (VF) and Pulseless Ventricular Tachycardia (pVT)
Mechanism & Goal: These rhythms involve chaotic, disorganized electrical activity without mechanical output. The core priority is immediate unsynchronized defibrillation (a high-energy shock). The shock simultaneously depolarizes a critical mass of the myocardium to instantly halt the chaotic activity, allowing the SA node to regain control.
Algorithm: Defibrillation is followed immediately by 2 minutes of high-quality CPR (do not pause to check the pulse after shocking). Epinephrine (1 mg IV/IO every 3–5 minutes) is administered after initial defibrillation attempts have failed. Amiodarone or lidocaine can be added for refractory VF/pVT.
Non-Shockable Rhythms: Asystole and Pulseless Electrical Activity (PEA)
Mechanism & Goal: PEA represents organized electrical activity that fails to generate a palpable mechanical pulse, while asystole is a complete absence of electrical and mechanical activity.
Algorithm: Defibrillating these rhythms is entirely ineffective, disrupts chest compressions, and wastes critical time. Management hinges entirely on immediate, uninterrupted CPR and the early administration of epinephrine (1 mg IV/IO every 3–5 minutes). Note: Routine atropine is no longer recommended for asystole or PEA.
Reversible Causes (Hs and Ts): The key to saving a patient with a non-shockable rhythm is simultaneously hunting for and treating the underlying cause.
5 Hs: Hypovolemia, Hypoxia, Hydrogen ion (acidosis), Hypokalemia/Hyperkalemia, Hypothermia.
5 Ts: Toxins (tablets/overdose), Tamponade (cardiac), Tension pneumothorax, Thrombosis (pulmonary embolism), Thrombosis (coronary/MI).

Initial Bedside Triage
The "First 60 Seconds" Survey The immediate bedside approach to any potential arrhythmia begins with a rapid primary survey focusing on the patient's airway, breathing, circulation, and mental state,. During this initial minute, the clinical team must obtain continuous rhythm monitoring, establish intravenous access, and record a 12-lead ECG while concurrently assessing the patient's perfusion phenotype (e.g., warm/cold, wet/dry) and volume status,.
Hemodynamic Stability Assessment The most critical branch point in arrhythmia management is determining if the rhythm is causing hemodynamic instability. A patient is considered unstable if the arrhythmia produces:
Hypotension or shock.
Altered consciousness.
Ongoing ischemic chest discomfort.
Acute pulmonary edema or severe heart failure.
Syncope or near-syncope.
If a tachyarrhythmia with a pulse is generating any of these high-risk features, immediate synchronized electrical cardioversion takes absolute priority over prolonged diagnostic analysis,.

ECG Diagnostic Algorithm
When the patient is stable, a structured step-by-step ECG interpretation helps define the arrhythmic mechanism.
QRS Width Evaluation The width of the QRS complex identifies the route of ventricular activation.
Narrow-Complex (\(\le\) 120 ms): Indicates the ventricles are being activated normally through the His-Purkinje system, confirming the rhythm originates above the ventricles (supraventricular).
Wide-Complex (> 120 ms): Indicates delayed or abnormal ventricular activation. This can result from a ventricular origin, supraventricular tachycardia (SVT) with aberrancy (bundle-branch block), or pre-excitation via an accessory pathway. Crucial Rule: An undifferentiated regular wide-complex tachycardia must be managed as ventricular tachycardia (VT) until proven otherwise, especially in patients with structural heart disease.
Rhythm Regularity Combining QRS width with rhythm regularity rapidly narrows the differential diagnosis,:
Regular Narrow: Sinus tachycardia, Atrioventricular Nodal Re-entrant Tachycardia (AVNRT), orthodromic AV-re-entrant tachycardia (AVRT), focal atrial tachycardia, or atrial flutter with a fixed block.
Irregular Narrow: Atrial fibrillation (AFib), atrial flutter with variable block, or multifocal atrial tachycardia.
Regular Wide: VT, SVT with aberrancy, or antidromic AVRT.
Irregular Wide: AFib with bundle-branch block, polymorphic VT, or pre-excited AFib (which is a medical emergency where AV-nodal blockers are strictly contraindicated),,.
Atrial-Ventricular Relationship Analyzing the P waves relative to the QRS complexes clarifies the origin and conduction pathway.
AV Dissociation: If P waves and QRS complexes march out independently at different rates, this strongly supports VT in the setting of a wide-complex tachycardia.
Retrograde or Hidden P waves: P waves buried within or appearing immediately after the QRS suggest AVNRT.
Dropped Beats: Progressive PR lengthening before a dropped QRS defines a Mobitz Type I block, whereas constant PR intervals with unexpectedly dropped QRS complexes define a more dangerous Mobitz Type II infranodal block,.

Focused Investigations
Laboratory Profiling Routine laboratory assessment is aimed at identifying reversible triggers or metabolic consequences of the arrhythmia. This includes checking electrolytes (specifically looking for hypokalemia or hypomagnesemia), renal function, and thyroid function. A complete blood count and glucose should be obtained, alongside drug levels for patients on antiarrhythmics or digoxin where toxicity is suspected. High-sensitivity cardiac troponin is evaluated if concurrent ischemia is suspected.
Ambulatory Monitoring and Imaging
Monitoring: Since resting ECGs may be normal between episodes, continuous monitoring is tailored to symptom frequency. A 24-hour Holter monitor may suffice for daily symptoms, whereas patch monitors, event recorders, or implantable loop recorders are indicated for infrequent, unexplained episodes like unpredictable syncope,.
Imaging: Echocardiography is the first-line imaging test to evaluate chamber size, valvular function, and baseline structural heart disease. Cardiac Magnetic Resonance (CMR) is utilized to characterize tissue, identifying myocardial inflammation, infiltrative processes, and mapping the precise pattern of non-ischemic or ischemic scar that may be serving as an arrhythmic substrate,,.

Cardiac Arrest Classification
Cardiac arrest is defined as the abrupt loss of effective circulation, identified clinically by unresponsiveness combined with absent or abnormal breathing (e.g., agonal gasps). The resuscitation algorithm is strictly dictated by whether the initial rhythm is shockable or non-shockable.
Shockable Rhythms
Rhythms: Ventricular Fibrillation (VF) and pulseless Ventricular Tachycardia (pVT).
Action: The immediate priority is unsynchronized defibrillation (a high-energy shock) to depolarize the chaotic myocardium and allow the sinus node to regain control,. This is followed instantly by high-quality CPR (30 compressions to 2 breaths in adults) for 2 minutes before pausing to recheck the rhythm,,.
Non-Shockable Rhythms
Rhythms: Asystole (a flatline indicating absent electrical and mechanical activity) and Pulseless Electrical Activity (PEA, where organized electrical activity occurs but fails to generate a palpable pulse).
Action: Defibrillation is ineffective and wastes critical time. Management relies entirely on immediate, continuous CPR and the earliest possible administration of epinephrine (1 mg IV/IO every 3-5 minutes),. Survival hinges on simultaneously diagnosing and treating the reversible "5 Hs and 5 Ts" (e.g., Hypovolemia, Hypoxia, Tension pneumothorax, Tamponade, Toxins, Thrombosis),.

Building upon our previous discussion of tachyarrhythmias and general electrophysiology, here is a focused breakdown of bradyarrhythmias, conduction failures, and the management of non-shockable cardiac arrest.
Pathophysiological Mechanisms Bradyarrhythmias arise from a failure in impulse formation, impulse conduction, or a combination of both.
Sinus Node Dysfunction (SND): The sinoatrial (SA) node normally dictates the heart rate because it reaches the threshold for spontaneous depolarization faster than other tissues. SND occurs when impulse formation or propagation from the SA node is impaired, frequently due to age-related fibrosis, ischemia, infiltrative diseases, or extrinsic suppressors like beta-blockers, hypothermia, and hypothyroidism.
Conduction Failure (AV Blocks): This occurs when impulse transmission is delayed or completely blocked within the atrioventricular (AV) node or the infranodal His-Purkinje system.
Ischemic correlation: The anatomical location of an infarct heavily dictates the nature of the block. An inferior myocardial infarction frequently compromises the AV nodal blood supply (often from the right coronary artery), leading to a transient, nodal block with a relatively narrow and reliable escape rhythm. Conversely, an anterior myocardial infarction damages the interventricular septum and His-Purkinje system, resulting in an infranodal block with a wide, unreliable ventricular escape rhythm and a much poorer prognosis.
Clinical Recognition
Signs of Instability: The absolute priority when assessing any arrhythmia is determining hemodynamic stability. A bradyarrhythmia is considered unstable if the patient exhibits hypotension/shock, altered mental status, ongoing ischemic chest discomfort, or acute heart failure.
Physical Exam Clues - Cannon A Waves: Inspection of the jugular venous pulse (JVP) provides direct insight into right atrial hemodynamics. Intermittent "cannon" A waves occur when the right atrium contracts forcefully against a closed tricuspid valve. This is a hallmark physical sign of atrioventricular dissociation (where the atria and ventricles are beating entirely independently of one another), commonly seen in complete (third-degree) heart block or ventricular tachycardia.
ECG Classification
First-Degree AV Block: A conduction delay resulting in a prolonged PR interval (>200 ms), but every atrial impulse (P wave) successfully conducts to the ventricles (QRS). It is often benign or drug-related.
Second-Degree AV Block (Mobitz Type I / Wenckebach): Characterized by progressive lengthening of the PR interval until an atrial impulse is completely blocked (a "dropped" QRS complex). The block is typically located within the AV node, and because the lower escape pacemakers usually remain reliable, it is generally lower-risk.
Second-Degree AV Block (Mobitz Type II): Characterized by constant PR intervals with unexpectedly dropped QRS complexes. This block is usually infranodal (His-Purkinje system) and is highly dangerous, as it carries a substantial risk of sudden progression to complete heart block.
Third-Degree (Complete) AV Block: There is absolutely no electrical communication between the atria and ventricles. P waves and QRS complexes occur regularly but at completely different rates (AV dissociation), with the atrial rate typically faster than the ventricular escape rate.
Sick Sinus Syndrome (Tachy-Brady Syndrome): A heterogeneous manifestation of SND where the ECG may show profound sinus bradycardia, sinus pauses, or sinus arrest. It frequently presents as "tachy-brady syndrome," where bursts of atrial tachyarrhythmias (like atrial fibrillation) alternate with prolonged, symptomatic bradycardic pauses upon termination of the tachycardia.
Acute Stabilization Pathway For patients with symptomatic or unstable bradycardia, the treatment escalates based on pharmacological response and the anatomical level of the block:
First-Line Pharmacology: Intravenous Atropine is the initial drug of choice. It works by removing vagal tone, making it effective for sinus bradycardia or AV nodal blocks. However, atropine is often ineffective for high-grade or complete AV blocks with a distal (infranodal) escape rhythm, because the diseased His-Purkinje tissue lies below the vagally responsive AV node.
Pharmacological Alternatives: If atropine fails, intravenous infusions of epinephrine or dopamine can be utilized to stimulate chronotropy and inotropy.
Pacing Escalation: If medications are ineffective or the patient is profoundly unstable, transcutaneous pacing is initiated immediately. This serves as a bridge to temporary transvenous pacing, which secures the heart rate until definitive therapy—typically the implantation of a permanent pacemaker—can be arranged.
Cardiac Arrest Considerations: Asystole and PEA Protocols Unlike Ventricular Fibrillation (VF) and pulseless Ventricular Tachycardia (pVT), which require immediate high-energy shocks, Asystole and Pulseless Electrical Activity (PEA) are non-shockable rhythms.
PEA Definition: The ECG displays organized electrical activity (which can look like normal sinus rhythm or bradycardia), but the heart fails to generate a palpable mechanical pulse. Asystole is a complete absence of electrical and mechanical activity (flatline).
Protocols: Defibrillation is strictly contraindicated as it is entirely ineffective and wastes critical time. Management relies entirely on immediate, uninterrupted cardiopulmonary resuscitation (CPR) and the earliest possible administration of epinephrine (1 mg IV/IO every 3–5 minutes). Notably, the routine use of atropine is no longer recommended for asystole or PEA.
Reversible Causes (5 Hs and 5 Ts): Survival in non-shockable arrest hinges on acting as a rapid diagnostician to find and treat the underlying trigger simultaneously.
5 Hs: Hypovolemia, Hypoxia, Hydrogen ion (acidosis), Hypokalemia/Hyperkalemia, Hypothermia.
5 Ts: Toxins (e.g., drug overdose), Tamponade (cardiac), Tension pneumothorax, Thrombosis (pulmonary embolism), Thrombosis (coronary/MI).

Initial Triage and Stability
Hemodynamic Stability Criteria When evaluating any tachyarrhythmia, the absolute first step is to determine if the abnormal rhythm is causing hemodynamic instability. A patient is considered unstable if the rapid rhythm produces:
Hypotension or shock.
Altered consciousness or mental status.
Ongoing ischemic chest discomfort or angina.
Acute pulmonary edema or severe heart failure.
Syncope or near-syncope (due to inadequate cardiac output).
Cardioversion vs. Defibrillation If a patient exhibits any of the above instability criteria while having a palpable pulse, the immediate treatment is synchronized electrical cardioversion. This delivers a low-energy shock specifically timed with the R wave of the QRS complex to avoid shocking during the T wave (the vulnerable repolarization phase), which could precipitate ventricular fibrillation (VF). Conversely, unsynchronized cardioversion (defibrillation) delivers a high-energy shock regardless of the cardiac cycle. It is strictly indicated for non-perfusing, "shockable" cardiac arrest rhythms: Ventricular Fibrillation (VF) and pulseless Ventricular Tachycardia (pVT), as well as unstable polymorphic VT where synchronization is impossible.

Mechanisms of Tachyarrhythmia
The underlying pathophysiology of tachyarrhythmias falls into three primary mechanisms:
Abnormal Automaticity: Myocardial cells that do not typically act as pacemakers develop spontaneous phase-4 depolarization. This is often driven by pathological states like ischemia, metabolic disturbances, or excessive catecholamines. These rhythms (e.g., accelerated idioventricular rhythm, multifocal atrial tachycardia) tend to accelerate and decelerate gradually.
Triggered Activity: This occurs due to oscillations in the membrane potential called afterdepolarizations, which happen during or immediately after an action potential.
Early afterdepolarizations happen during phases 2 or 3 and are exacerbated by bradycardia, hypokalemia, and prolonged QT intervals (leading to Torsades de pointes).
Delayed afterdepolarizations occur in phase 4, usually resulting from intracellular calcium overload (e.g., digoxin toxicity, heart failure, catecholamine excess).
Re-entry: The most common tachyarrhythmia mechanism. It requires two distinct conduction pathways forming a circuit with differing refractory periods and conduction speeds. A premature impulse encounters unidirectional block in one pathway, travels down the other, and returns retrogradely up the first, establishing a continuous, rapid circular wavefront. Re-entry explains rhythms that start and stop abruptly, such as AVNRT, AVRT, and scar-related VT.

ECG Diagnostic Grid
Evaluating QRS width and rhythm regularity rapidly narrows the differential diagnosis:
Narrow-Complex (\(\le\) 120 ms): Indicates ventricular activation is occurring normally via the His-Purkinje system, meaning the origin is supraventricular.
Regular: Sinus tachycardia, Atrioventricular Nodal Re-entrant Tachycardia (AVNRT), orthodromic AV-re-entrant tachycardia (AVRT), focal atrial tachycardia, atrial flutter with a fixed block (e.g., 2:1 conduction).
Irregular: Atrial fibrillation (AFib), atrial flutter with variable block, multifocal atrial tachycardia (MAT).
Wide-Complex (> 120 ms): Indicates delayed or abnormal ventricular activation. Crucial Rule: Any regular wide-complex tachycardia in an adult with structural heart disease must be treated as Ventricular Tachycardia (VT) until proven otherwise.
Regular: Ventricular tachycardia, Supraventricular Tachycardia (SVT) with aberrancy (bundle-branch block), antidromic AVRT, or a paced ventricular rhythm.
Irregular: AFib with bundle-branch block, pre-excited AFib (AFib conducting down an accessory pathway), or polymorphic VT (including Torsades de pointes).

Specific Arrhythmias
Atrial Fibrillation (AFib) and Flutter
AFib: The most common sustained arrhythmia, characterized by disorganized atrial electrical activity originating near the pulmonary vein ostia. The ECG shows an irregularly irregular rhythm with an undulating baseline and absent P waves. Management hinges on rate control (beta-blockers, non-dihydropyridine CCBs, digoxin) or rhythm control (cardioversion, antiarrhythmics, catheter ablation). Furthermore, patients require stroke risk stratification (e.g., CHA2DS2-VASc score) to determine the need for long-term oral anticoagulation.
Atrial Flutter: A macro-re-entrant circuit typically circulating around the right atrial tricuspid annulus. It produces a regular "sawtooth" pattern of continuous flutter waves on the ECG, often with a 2:1 AV block resulting in a ventricular rate near 150 bpm.
Paroxysmal Supraventricular Tachycardias (AVNRT/AVRT) These present with sudden-onset palpitations and regular, narrow QRS complexes.
AVNRT: The re-entry circuit is localized entirely within the AV node, utilizing a fast and a slow pathway. Atria and ventricles depolarize almost simultaneously, often hiding P waves within the QRS or producing pseudo-R' waves in V1.
AVRT: Driven by an accessory pathway outside the AV node. In its orthodromic form, the impulse travels down the AV node and retrogradely up the accessory pathway, causing a narrow complex.
Wolff-Parkinson-White (WPW) Syndrome A pre-excitation syndrome caused by an abnormal fast accessory pathway (Bundle of Kent) bypassing the AV node. The baseline ECG shows a short PR interval, a slurred QRS upstroke (delta wave), and a widened QRS.
Danger: If a patient with WPW develops Pre-excited AFib (an irregular, very rapid, wide-complex tachycardia), the accessory pathway can conduct uninhibited atrial impulses directly to the ventricles, deteriorating into VFib. AV nodal blocking agents (beta-blockers, CCBs, adenosine, digoxin) are strictly contraindicated as they force all conduction down the accessory pathway.
Ventricular Tachycardia (VT) and Torsades de Pointes
VT: A regular, wide-complex tachycardia most commonly arising from a re-entry circuit within scar tissue from a prior myocardial infarction. Three or more consecutive PVCs define VT; if it lasts >30 seconds or causes instability, it is sustained.
Torsades de Pointes: A specific polymorphic VT characterized by QRS complexes twisting around the isoelectric baseline. It is precipitated by a prolonged QT interval caused by drugs (Class IA/III antiarrhythmics, macrolides, haloperidol, tricyclic antidepressants), hypokalemia, hypomagnesemia, or congenital long QT syndromes (e.g., Romano-Ward).
Ventricular Fibrillation (VF) A completely chaotic, totally erratic wide-complex tracing with no discernible P, QRS, or T waves. It produces no cardiac output, causing sudden cardiac death. Immediate unsynchronized defibrillation and CPR are mandatory.

Antiarrhythmic Pharmacology
Vaughan Williams Classes I-IV
Class I (Sodium-Channel Blockers): Target phase 0 of the action potential. They are state-dependent, binding more strongly at faster heart rates.
Class IA (Quinidine, Procainamide, Disopyramide): Moderate Na+ blockade. They prolong the action potential (AP) and effective refractory period (ERP), increasing the QT interval (risk of Torsades).
Class IB (Lidocaine, Phenytoin, Mexiletine): Weak Na+ blockade. They shorten AP duration and preferentially target ischemic/depolarized Purkinje tissue, making them excellent for post-MI arrhythmias.
Class IC (Flecainide, Propafenone): Strong Na+ blockade with minimal effect on AP duration. Strongly proarrhythmic and absolutely contraindicated in patients with structural or ischemic heart disease.
Class II (Beta-Blockers): (e.g., Metoprolol, Esmolol). Decrease SA and AV nodal activity by lowering cAMP and calcium currents, reducing the slope of phase 4 spontaneous depolarization.
Class III (Potassium-Channel Blockers): (e.g., Amiodarone, Sotalol, Dofetilide, Ibutilide). Prolong phase 3 repolarization, greatly increasing AP duration and the QT interval. Sotalol and ibutilide carry high risks for Torsades de pointes; amiodarone has a lower Torsades risk but massive systemic toxicities (pulmonary fibrosis, thyroid dysfunction, hepatotoxicity).
Class IV (Calcium-Channel Blockers): Non-dihydropyridines (Verapamil, Diltiazem). Block L-type calcium channels, significantly decreasing conduction velocity and increasing the refractory period at the AV node.
Adenosine and Magnesium
Adenosine: An ultra-short-acting agent (~15 seconds) that drives potassium out of cells, hyperpolarizing the membrane and decreasing calcium influx to rapidly block the AV node. It is the drug of choice for diagnosing and terminating stable AV-nodal-dependent SVTs. Its effects are blunted by caffeine and theophylline.
Magnesium: The highly effective first-line treatment for resolving Torsades de pointes and digoxin toxicity arrhythmias.

Pathophysiology Atrial fibrillation (AF) is the most common sustained cardiac arrhythmia, characterized by uncoordinated atrial electrical activation and ineffective atrial contraction,.
Pulmonary Vein Triggers: The arrhythmia is frequently initiated by rapidly and irregularly depolarizing cells located near the ostia of the pulmonary veins in the left atrium,.
Remodeling: Once triggered, AF is maintained by a vulnerable atrial substrate driven by multiple re-entrant wavelets, atrial dilatation, fibrosis, and conduction heterogeneity. This electrical and structural remodeling creates a self-reinforcing cycle where "atrial fibrillation begets atrial fibrillation".
Loss of Atrial Kick: The uncoordinated fibrillatory activity eliminates effective atrial contraction, depriving the ventricles of their late-diastolic "atrial kick". This loss of preload significantly impairs ventricular filling, which is especially detrimental in older patients or those with stiff, non-compliant ventricles (such as in left ventricular hypertrophy or restrictive physiology).
Ventricular Consequences: The rapid and irregular ventricular response shortens the diastolic filling time, reducing coronary perfusion while simultaneously increasing myocardial oxygen demand,. If left uncontrolled, the persistently rapid rate can lead to tachycardia-induced cardiomyopathy, resulting in progressive left ventricular dilatation and systolic dysfunction,.
Classification AF is classified chronologically based on its temporal pattern and the selected treatment strategy:
First Diagnosed: The initial documented episode of AF, regardless of its duration or the presence of symptoms,.
Paroxysmal: An episode that is self-limited, terminating spontaneously or with medical intervention within 7 days of onset,,.
Persistent: An episode that is continuously sustained beyond 7 days, or an episode that requires electrical or pharmacological cardioversion to terminate,.
Long-standing Persistent: Continuous AF that has lasted for at least 12 months in a patient for whom rhythm-control strategies are still actively being pursued,,.
Permanent: This classification represents a shared therapeutic decision between the patient and the clinician to accept the presence of AF and abandon further attempts to restore sinus rhythm,,. It describes a treatment strategy rather than an immutable biological state, and can be reconsidered if circumstances change.
Clinical Recognition
Symptoms and Signs: The clinical presentation ranges from completely asymptomatic to severe palpitations, dyspnea, fatigue, chest discomfort, dizziness, or syncope,. On physical examination, the hallmark finding is an irregularly irregular pulse and irregular jugular venous pulsations. A "pulse deficit" may also be appreciated, which occurs when rapid, inefficient ventricular contractions fail to generate a stroke volume large enough to produce a palpable peripheral pulse.
ECG Characteristics: A 12-lead ECG is required to definitively establish the diagnosis. The classic ECG features include the absence of consistent, discrete P waves, an undulating or fibrillatory baseline, and an irregularly irregular ventricular response (variable RR intervals), provided atrioventricular (AV) conduction is intact,,.
Management Strategy (AF-CARE) The modern framework for managing AF relies on the comprehensive AF-CARE pathway,:
C - Comorbidity and risk-factor management: Because systemic diseases drive atrial remodeling, effective management requires aggressive treatment of hypertension, heart failure, diabetes, obesity, sleep apnea, and excessive alcohol consumption,.
A - Avoid stroke and thromboembolism: Ineffective atrial contraction causes blood stasis, particularly within the left atrial appendage, predisposing the patient to thrombus formation and embolic stroke,. Stroke risk is objectively quantified using the CHA2DS2-VA or CHA2DS2-VASc score, assigning points for Congestive heart failure, Hypertension, Age \(\ge\)75 (2 points), Diabetes, Stroke/TIA/thromboembolism (2 points), Vascular disease, Age 65-74, and female Sex,.
Anticoagulation: Oral anticoagulation is strongly recommended for a score of \(\ge\)2 in men and \(\ge\)3 in women. Direct oral anticoagulants (DOACs) are generally preferred over vitamin K antagonists (warfarin) due to a lower risk of intracranial bleeding. However, warfarin is strictly required for valvular AF (patients with mechanical prosthetic valves or moderate-to-severe rheumatic mitral stenosis),,. If a patient is at high stroke risk but has an absolute contraindication to anticoagulation, minimally invasive percutaneous left atrial appendage occlusion can be considered.
R - Reduce symptoms by rate and rhythm control:
Rate Control: Accepts the fibrillatory rhythm but uses AV-nodal blocking agents (beta-blockers, diltiazem, verapamil, or digoxin) to slow the ventricular response, typically targeting a resting heart rate <110 bpm,,. Note that non-dihydropyridine calcium channel blockers (verapamil/diltiazem) must be avoided in decompensated heart failure with reduced ejection fraction (HFrEF).
Rhythm Control: Aims to restore and maintain normal sinus rhythm to relieve symptoms, prevent adverse remodeling, and improve quality of life,,. Options include electrical cardioversion, antiarrhythmic drugs (e.g., flecainide, propafenone, dofetilide, amiodarone), and catheter ablation,. Catheter ablation electrically isolates the arrhythmogenic pulmonary veins from the rest of the left atrium and is highly effective for symptomatic patients, particularly when drug therapy fails or when AF induces heart failure,.
E - Evaluation and dynamic reassessment: Long-term success requires periodic reassessment of symptoms, stroke/bleeding risks, medication adherence, and structural disease progression.
Cardiac Arrest and Emergency Contexts
Hemodynamic Instability: If a patient with AF presents with shock, persistent hypotension, altered mental status, ongoing ischemic chest pain, or acute pulmonary edema, immediate synchronized electrical cardioversion is mandated,,. Diagnostic testing must never delay electrical therapy in an unstable patient.
Pre-excited AF (WPW Syndrome): Wolff-Parkinson-White (WPW) syndrome involves an accessory electrical pathway bypassing the AV node. If a patient with WPW develops AF, the irregular atrial impulses can travel directly down the accessory pathway,. Because the accessory pathway lacks the rate-limiting, decremental properties of the normal AV node, the ventricles are exposed to extremely rapid rates (often >200 bpm), producing an irregular, wide-complex tachycardia,.
The Danger: This hyper-rapid conduction carries a severe risk of deteriorating into fatal ventricular fibrillation (VF),.
Absolute Contraindications: AV-nodal blocking agents (beta-blockers, diltiazem, verapamil, digoxin, adenosine, and IV amiodarone) are strictly contraindicated,,. Giving these medications paralyzes the AV node and forces all fibrillatory conduction down the accessory pathway, precipitating cardiac arrest.
Management: Unstable pre-excited AF requires immediate synchronized cardioversion. Stable patients require specialized pathway-safe antiarrhythmic therapy (such as procainamide). Definitive treatment is catheter ablation of the accessory pathway.

Electrical Therapies
Synchronized Cardioversion: This therapy delivers a low-energy shock specifically timed to synchronize with the R wave of the QRS complex. By doing so, it avoids delivering energy during the T wave (the vulnerable repolarization phase), which could inadvertently precipitate ventricular fibrillation (VF). It is strictly used to convert abnormal, yet perfusing, rhythms back to sinus rhythm, and is indicated for hemodynamically unstable supraventricular tachycardia (SVT), atrial fibrillation (AF), atrial flutter, and monomorphic ventricular tachycardia (VT) with a pulse.
Unsynchronized Defibrillation: This is an urgent, high-energy shock delivered independently of the cardiac cycle. It simultaneously depolarizes a critical mass of myocardium to instantly halt chaotic electrical activity, allowing normal pacemaker tissue to regain control. It is strictly indicated for non-perfusing, shockable rhythms: Ventricular Fibrillation (VF) and pulseless VT. It is not used for asystole or pulseless electrical activity (PEA).
Pacing and Implantable Devices:
Temporary Pacing: Used as a bridge through acute or unstable periods. Transcutaneous pacing utilizes adhesive chest electrodes and can be initiated rapidly, but may be uncomfortable or unreliable. Transvenous pacing advances a catheter into the right heart, providing reliable support but carrying risks of infection, vascular injury, or perforation.
Permanent Pacemakers: Indicated for symptomatic sinus-node dysfunction, Mobitz Type II AV block, or complete (third-degree) AV block that is not reversible.
Implantable Cardioverter-Defibrillators (ICDs): Continuously monitor the ventricular rhythm to prevent sudden cardiac death. They terminate life-threatening arrhythmias via antitachycardia pacing or shocks. They are indicated for secondary prevention in survivors of cardiac arrest/sustained VT, and for primary prevention in asymptomatic patients with a persistently reduced left ventricular ejection fraction (LVEF \(\le\) 30-35%) despite optimal medical therapy.
Cardiac Resynchronization Therapy (CRT): Also known as biventricular pacing, this restores coordinated right and left ventricular contraction. It is highly beneficial for symptomatic heart failure patients with reduced LVEF (\(\le\) 35%), sinus rhythm, and a widened QRS complex (particularly left bundle-branch block morphology \(\ge\) 150 ms).
Vaughan Williams Classification
Class I (Sodium-Channel Blockers): These drugs slow phase 0 rapid depolarization of the action potential. Their blockade is "state-dependent," meaning they bind more strongly at faster heart rates.
Class IA (Quinidine, Procainamide, Disopyramide): Provide moderate Na+ blockade, prolong the action potential and effective refractory period (ERP), and increase the QT interval (carrying a risk for Torsades de pointes).
Class IB (Lidocaine, Phenytoin, Mexiletine): Provide weak Na+ blockade and uniquely shorten the action potential duration. They preferentially target ischemic and depolarized Purkinje tissue, making them the best option for acute post-MI ventricular arrhythmias.
Class IC (Flecainide, Propafenone): Provide strong Na+ blockade and markedly prolong the ERP in the AV node without affecting overall action potential duration. Because they are highly proarrhythmic, they are strictly contraindicated in patients with structural or ischemic heart disease.
Class II (Beta-Blockers): Examples include metoprolol, propranolol, and esmolol. They decrease SA and AV nodal automaticity and conduction by reducing cAMP and intracellular calcium currents, suppressing abnormal pacemakers by decreasing the slope of phase 4 spontaneous depolarization. They are highly useful for SVTs, rate control in AF, and preventing ventricular arrhythmias post-MI.
Class III (Potassium-Channel Blockers): Examples include amiodarone, sotalol, dofetilide, and ibutilide. They prolong phase 3 rapid repolarization, which greatly increases the action potential duration, the ERP, and the QT interval. While sotalol and ibutilide carry a high risk of provoking Torsades de pointes, amiodarone (which has properties of all four classes) has a lower Torsades risk but is limited by massive systemic toxicities, including pulmonary fibrosis, hepatotoxicity, and thyroid dysfunction.
Class IV (Calcium-Channel Blockers): Specifically the non-dihydropyridines (verapamil and diltiazem). They block L-type calcium channels, significantly decreasing conduction velocity and increasing the refractory period at the AV node. They are excellent for SVTs and rate control, but can worsen decompensated heart failure and are contraindicated in undifferentiated wide-complex tachycardias.
Miscellaneous Antiarrhythmics
Adenosine: An ultra-short-acting agent (\(\sim\) 15 seconds) that drives potassium out of cells, hyperpolarizing the membrane and decreasing calcium influx. This rapidly blocks AV nodal conduction. It is the drug of choice for diagnosing and terminating stable, AV-node-dependent SVTs. Its effects are blunted by adenosine receptor antagonists like caffeine and theophylline, and it should never be given for irregular wide-complex tachycardias.
Digoxin: Directly inhibits the Na+/K+-ATPase pump, which indirectly impairs the Na+/Ca2+ exchanger. This traps calcium inside the cell, causing increased myocardial contractility (positive inotropy). It also stimulates the vagus nerve to slow AV nodal conduction. Toxicity causes cholinergic symptoms (nausea, vomiting), visual disturbances (yellow vision), and arrhythmias, and is dangerously exacerbated by hypokalemia.
Magnesium: The highly effective, first-line intravenous therapy for terminating Torsades de pointes and arrhythmias driven by digoxin toxicity.
Cardiac Arrest Pharmacology
Epinephrine: The standard vasopressor in cardiac arrest, administered at a dose of 1 mg every 3 to 5 minutes. In non-shockable rhythms (asystole and PEA), it must be administered as early as feasible alongside continuous CPR. In shockable rhythms (VF/pulseless VT), its administration is prioritized only after initial rapid defibrillation attempts have failed.
Amiodarone and Lidocaine: These antiarrhythmic agents are considered specifically for refractory Ventricular Fibrillation (VF) or pulseless Ventricular Tachycardia (pVT) that remains unresponsive to repeated cycles of CPR and defibrillation.

Basic Life Support (BLS): Recognition and High-Quality Metrics While we have previously discussed the pharmacological management of cardiac arrest, survival fundamentally depends on the immediate execution of high-quality Basic Life Support (BLS).
Recognition and Initiation: Cardiac arrest is identified by unresponsiveness coupled with absent or abnormal breathing (such as agonal gasps, which are not normal breathing). Rescuers must initiate chest compressions immediately if a definite pulse cannot be felt within 10 seconds.
High-Quality Compression Metrics:
Rate and Depth: Compressions must be delivered at a rate of 100 to 120 per minute, with a depth of at least 5 cm but avoiding excessive depths greater than 6 cm in average adults.
Mechanics: Hands should be positioned on the lower half of the sternum on a firm surface. Crucially, rescuers must allow complete chest recoil after each compression, avoid leaning on the chest, and minimize pauses to ensure a high chest-compression fraction.
Ventilation Ratio: Without an advanced airway, the compression-to-ventilation ratio is 30:2, delivering each breath over approximately 1 second with visible chest rise. Once an advanced airway is placed, compressions become continuous, accompanied by 1 breath every 6 seconds without pausing compressions (avoiding hyperventilation).
Rotation: To prevent fatigue-induced drops in compression quality, the compressor should be rotated approximately every 2 minutes.
Advanced Life Support (ALS) Rhythm Categories As established in our previous discussions, ALS algorithms strictly bifurcate based on the underlying electrical mechanism:
Shockable Rhythms: Ventricular Fibrillation (VF) and pulseless Ventricular Tachycardia (pVT) demand immediate defibrillation combined with uninterrupted CPR cycles.
Non-Shockable Rhythms: Asystole and Pulseless Electrical Activity (PEA) do not benefit from defibrillation. They require continuous CPR, early pharmacological support, and an aggressive search for reversible causes.
Defibrillation and Pharmacology Building on our previous review of the treatment toolbox, ALS incorporates specific dosing and timing parameters during an arrest:
Energy Selection: For shockable rhythms, unsynchronized defibrillation should utilize biphasic energy at the manufacturer's recommended setting. If the recommended setting is unknown, the maximum available energy setting should be used. If a monophasic defibrillator is used, the dose is 360 Joules.
Epinephrine Timing: The standard dose is 1 mg administered IV/IO every 3 to 5 minutes. The timing depends on the rhythm: in non-shockable arrest (Asystole/PEA), it should be administered as early as feasible. In shockable arrest (VF/pVT), it is prioritized only after initial defibrillation attempts have failed.
Refractory Arrhythmia Agents: If VF or pVT remains refractory to repeated shocks and CPR, Amiodarone or Lidocaine may be considered to help restore an organized rhythm.
Reversible Causes (The 5 Hs and 5 Ts) To achieve Return of Spontaneous Circulation (ROSC) in any arrest, particularly non-shockable rhythms, clinicians must simultaneously hunt for and reverse the underlying triggers.
5 Hs: Hypovolemia, Hypoxia, Hydrogen ion (acidosis), Hypokalemia/Hyperkalemia, and Hypothermia.
5 Ts: Tension pneumothorax, Tamponade (cardiac), Toxins (e.g., overdose), Thrombosis (pulmonary embolism), and Thrombosis (coronary/MI).
Post-Cardiac Arrest Care Achieving ROSC is only the first step; the post-arrest phase requires meticulous critical care to protect the brain and stabilize the myocardium.
Hemodynamic Targets: Hypotension must be strictly avoided. The mean arterial pressure (MAP) should be maintained at a minimum of 65 mmHg, though this target should be individualized upwards based on the patient's baseline and clinical requirements.
Oxygenation and Ventilation: Upon ROSC, 100% oxygen is used until reliable pulse oximetry is available. Thereafter, oxygen must be titrated to target an SpO2 of 90–98%, carefully avoiding both hypoxemia and hyperoxemia (which exacerbates reperfusion injury). Ventilation should target physiologic carbon dioxide levels (normocapnia), explicitly avoiding routine hyperventilation.
Targeted Temperature Management (TTM): If the patient remains unresponsive (comatose) after ROSC, protocolized temperature control is mandatory to provide neuroprotection. Modern guidelines have shifted from strictly targeting 32–34°C for 12–24 hours to maintaining a tightly controlled temperature between 32°C and 37.5°C for at least 36 hours, with a strict emphasis on preventing fever.
Coronary and Neurological Care: Urgent coronary angiography is indicated if there is persistent ST-elevation or strong suspicion of acute coronary occlusion, though routine immediate angiography is not required for all comatose patients. Neurological care involves treating seizures and employing delayed, multimodal prognostication once sedatives and confounders have cleared.
