Coronary Anatomy and Perfusion
Arterial Territories The coronary circulation is delivered via the left and right coronary arteries, which arise from the aortic sinuses just above the aortic valve.
Left Coronary Artery (LCA): The left main coronary artery typically divides into the left anterior descending (LAD) and the left circumflex (LCx) arteries. The LAD supplies the anterior two-thirds of the interventricular septum, the anterolateral papillary muscle, the anterior surface of the left ventricle (LV), and usually the cardiac apex. The LCx travels in the left atrioventricular groove, supplying the lateral and posterolateral walls of the LV, as well as variable portions of the inferior and posterior territories.
Right Coronary Artery (RCA): The RCA runs in the right atrioventricular groove and gives off acute marginal and nodal branches. It predominantly supplies the right ventricle (RV), the right atrium, the inferior LV wall, the posterior one-third of the interventricular septum, the posteromedial papillary muscle, and the sinoatrial and atrioventricular nodes.
Coronary Dominance Coronary dominance is strictly defined by which artery gives rise to the posterior descending artery (PDA), which supplies the posterior circulation.
Right Dominance: Present in the majority of individuals, where the PDA arises from the RCA.
Left Dominance: The PDA arises from the LCx.
Codominance: The posterior and inferior myocardial territories are supplied by branches from both the RCA and the LCx. Dominance is clinically vital because it dictates the extent of the myocardial territory at risk during an occlusion; for instance, a proximal RCA occlusion in a right-dominant system threatens both the inferior LV and the AV node, commonly resulting in heart block.
Perfusion Physiology Because systolic myocardial contraction physically compresses intramyocardial vessels, coronary perfusion to the left ventricle occurs predominantly during diastole. Right-coronary flow is less dependent on diastole because the RV generates lower systolic pressures and wall stress, allowing for a more continuous flow.

Oxygen Supply and Demand
Ischemic heart disease occurs when myocardial oxygen demand exceeds the delivery capacity of the coronary circulation.
Variables Reducing Oxygen Supply Myocardial oxygen supply is determined by arterial oxygen content and coronary blood flow. Supply is reduced by:
Coronary Obstruction: Fixed atherosclerotic narrowing, dynamic vasospasm, or spontaneous dissection restricts maximal flow.
Reduced Diastolic Time: Tachycardia shortens the duration of diastole, critically limiting the time available for left ventricular perfusion.
Decreased Perfusion Pressure: Hypotension (low aortic diastolic pressure) or elevated left-ventricular end-diastolic pressure narrows the driving gradient for coronary blood flow.
Microvascular Dysfunction: Capillary rarefaction, endothelial dysfunction, or impaired arteriolar vasodilation can blunt the coronary flow reserve even without large-vessel disease.
Reduced Oxygen Content: Severe hypoxemia or significant anemia can precipitate ischemia by limiting the actual oxygen carried in the blood, independent of vessel patency.
Variables Increasing Oxygen Demand Myocardial oxygen consumption is driven by three main factors: heart rate, myocardial contractility, and ventricular wall stress. Demand is increased by:
Tachycardia: A rapid heart rate increases the number of energy-consuming contractions per minute, acting as a double-edged sword that simultaneously spikes demand while reducing supply time.
Increased Wall Stress/Afterload: According to Laplace's law, wall stress rises with increased intracavitary pressure and chamber radius. Conditions like chronic hypertension, aortic stenosis, and ventricular dilation force the heart to generate higher pressures, thereby surging oxygen requirements.
Increased Contractility: Sympathetic stimulation, exercise, or inotropic agents enhance the force of contraction, which utilizes more adenosine triphosphate (ATP).
Hypermetabolic States: Fever, thyrotoxicosis, or severe physical exertion drive systemic and cardiac metabolic rates upward.

Atherosclerosis Pathogenesis
Endothelial Dysfunction A healthy endothelium releases nitric oxide and prostacyclin, which promote vasodilation and inhibit platelet activation and smooth-muscle proliferation. Cardiovascular risk factors (e.g., smoking, hypertension, diabetes, dyslipidemia) degrade this environment, increasing oxidative stress and reducing nitric oxide bioavailability. The dysfunctional endothelium becomes highly permeable, promotes vasoconstriction, and expresses adhesion molecules that attract inflammatory cells and platelets.
Plaque Formation Apolipoprotein B-containing particles, notably low-density lipoprotein (LDL), penetrate the permeable endothelium and become trapped by proteoglycans in the arterial intima. Here, the lipids undergo oxidation and modification. In response to endothelial chemotactic signals, circulating monocytes adhere, migrate into the intima, and transform into macrophages. These macrophages relentlessly consume the modified lipids via scavenger receptors, turning into lipid-laden "foam cells". Ongoing inflammation forces vascular smooth-muscle cells to migrate from the tunica media to the intima, where they proliferate and deposit collagen and elastin. This forms a fibrous cap overlying a growing necrotic core composed of dead cells, cholesterol crystals, and debris.
Plaque Vulnerability and Thrombosis Plaques can be classified by their mechanical stability. A stable plaque possesses a thick fibrous cap, a small lipid core, and a prominent smooth-muscle presence, typically causing predictable exertional angina through luminal narrowing. Conversely, a vulnerable plaque features a massive necrotic core, intense macrophage-driven inflammation, and a dangerously thin fibrous cap.
Acute coronary syndromes occur when these vulnerable plaques fail:
Rupture: Macrophage-derived proteases weaken the fibrous cap until it tears at its shoulder, exposing the highly thrombogenic necrotic core and tissue factor to the bloodstream. This triggers the coagulation cascade and massive platelet activation.
Erosion: The endothelial layer sloughs off without a deep cap tear, exposing the subendothelial matrix, which also provokes a platelet-rich thrombus. Activated platelets release thromboxane A2 and ADP, aggregating via glycoprotein IIb/IIIa receptors, while thrombin converts fibrinogen to fibrin to cement the clot. This dynamic thrombus can completely occlude the artery, embolize distally, or cause superimposed vasospasm, leading to myocardial infarction.

Myocardial Infarction Evolution
When a coronary artery is persistently occluded, profound ischemia leads to irreversible cardiomyocyte death, typically initiating in the highly vulnerable subendocardium and progressing outward toward the epicardium (transmural infarction). The gross (macroscopic) and microscopic changes evolve predictably over time.
0 to 24 Hours
Macroscopic Changes: The tissue shows dark mottling and appears pale when exposed to tetrazolium stain.
Microscopic Changes: Between 0 to 4 hours, the myocardium demonstrates wavy fibers. From 4 to 24 hours, early coagulative necrosis begins, cellular contents are released into the blood, and local edema and hemorrhage appear. If reperfusion occurs, free radical damage and calcium influx can cause hypercontraction of myofibrils, visible as dark eosinophilic "contraction bands".
Key Complications: Malignant ventricular arrhythmias, heart failure, and cardiogenic shock.
1 to 3 Days
Macroscopic Changes: The infarcted area becomes overtly hyperemic.
Microscopic Changes: Extensive coagulative necrosis is present, and an acute inflammatory response brings a heavy infiltrate of neutrophils to the tissue surrounding the infarct.
Key Complications: Postinfarction fibrinous pericarditis.
3 to 14 Days
Macroscopic Changes: The infarct develops a hyperemic border with central yellow-brown softening.
Microscopic Changes: Macrophages arrive to scavenge necrotic debris, followed by the appearance of vascularized granulation tissue advancing at the margins.
Key Complications: Because macrophages structurally degrade the tissue to clear it, the ventricular wall is at its weakest. This is the peak window for free-wall rupture (causing tamponade), papillary muscle rupture (causing acute mitral regurgitation), and interventricular septal rupture (causing a left-to-right shunt).
2 Weeks to Several Months
Macroscopic Changes: The area matures into a firm, gray-white scar.
Microscopic Changes: The healing process culminates in a complete, contracted, dense collagenous scar.
Key Complications: Dressler syndrome (autoimmune pericarditis), heart failure, arrhythmias arising from scar boundary circuits, and true ventricular aneurysms (which carry a risk of mural thrombus).

Pathophysiological Basis Building on the principles of oxygen supply and demand and plaque biology discussed previously, Chronic Coronary Syndromes (CCS) occur when these mechanisms produce a stable, predictable pattern of ischemia.
Plaque Stability vs. Vulnerability: While vulnerable plaques are prone to acute rupture and thrombosis (causing Acute Coronary Syndromes), CCS is typically driven by a stable, fibrotic atherosclerotic plaque. This fixed epicardial narrowing may allow for adequate myocardial blood flow at rest but critically limits the coronary flow reserve, restricting the heart's ability to increase perfusion when exertion or stress drives up oxygen demand.
Oxygen Supply and Demand: In CCS, symptoms reliably appear when myocardial oxygen demand (driven by heart rate, contractility, and wall stress) exceeds the maximal flow deliverable across the fixed stenosis. The ischemia is generally transient and subendocardial, resolving once the demand drops (e.g., via rest) or supply improves.
Coronary Steal Physiology: Distal to a significant coronary stenosis, the microvascular resistance vessels are often maximally dilated at baseline to maintain adequate resting blood flow. If potent coronary vasodilators (such as adenosine, dipyridamole, or regadenoson) are administered, they massively dilate normal, healthy vessels. This creates a path of least resistance that shunts or "steals" blood away from the diseased, already-dilated vascular beds, thereby provoking detectable localized ischemia. This physiological quirk is harnessed diagnostically in pharmacological stress testing.
Clinical Recognition
Typical vs. Atypical Angina: Angina is classified based on three classic criteria: 1) Retrosternal chest discomfort (often described as pressure, squeezing, or heaviness), 2) Provocation by physical exertion or emotional stress, and 3) Relief by rest or short-acting nitrates within minutes.
Typical angina meets all three of these characteristics.
Atypical angina meets only two of the three.
Non-cardiac chest pain meets one or none of the criteria. Atypical or entirely painless presentations—such as isolated exertional dyspnea, unusual fatigue, nausea, or epigastric discomfort—are particularly common in women, older adults, and patients with diabetes or chronic kidney disease.
Canadian Cardiovascular Society (CCS) Grading: This system grades the functional limitation caused by angina:
Class I: Ordinary activity does not cause angina; symptoms only occur with rapid, prolonged, or strenuous exertion.
Class II: Slight limitation of ordinary activity; angina occurs when walking rapidly, climbing stairs after meals, or under emotional stress.
Class III: Marked limitation; angina occurs with walking just one or two blocks or climbing a single flight of stairs.
Class IV: Inability to carry out minimal physical activity without discomfort, or angina occurring at rest.
Diagnostic Testing
Resting ECG and Biomarkers: A resting 12-lead ECG may reveal signs of a prior infarction, baseline ST-T abnormalities, or conduction disease, but it is frequently completely normal between ischemic attacks. Similarly, high-sensitivity cardiac troponin levels are usually normal in stable CCS; troponin is not a screening tool for stable angina but is crucial for ruling out acute myocardial injury or ACS when symptoms worsen.
Stress Testing and Imaging: These tests expose flow reserve limitations by increasing myocardial demand or via pharmacological vasodilation.
Exercise ECG: Looks for horizontal or downsloping ST-segment depression; however, it has lower diagnostic accuracy if the baseline ECG is already abnormal.
Stress Echocardiography/Nuclear Imaging: Imaging-based tests offer higher specificity. Stress echo looks for new or worsening regional wall-motion abnormalities during ischemia. Nuclear myocardial perfusion imaging identifies reversible perfusion defects (ischemia) versus fixed defects (scar). Note that vasodilator stress agents (dipyridamole, adenosine) are contraindicated in patients with active asthma/bronchospasm, severe bradycardia or heart block, and hypotension.
Coronary CT Angiography (CCTA): Provides high-resolution, non-invasive anatomical visualization of the coronary lumen and plaque burden, making it highly effective for excluding obstructive CAD in appropriately selected patients.
Invasive Coronary Angiography: This is the reference standard for defining coronary anatomy, though it is invasive. It is generally reserved for patients with severe/refractory symptoms, high-risk non-invasive test results, or situations where revascularization is actively planned. Fractional Flow Reserve (FFR) can be measured during angiography to determine the true physiological significance of intermediate lesions.
Management Strategy Management combines immediate symptom relief with long-term prevention of atherothrombotic events.
Event Prevention (Disease-Modifying): Therapy aims to halt plaque progression and prevent thrombosis. This includes aggressive lifestyle modifications (smoking cessation, weight management), high-intensity statins to lower LDL cholesterol, and single-antiplatelet therapy (typically Aspirin) unless the patient has another indication for dual therapy. ACE inhibitors or ARBs are highly recommended, particularly if the patient has coexisting hypertension, diabetes, chronic kidney disease, or left ventricular dysfunction.
Symptom Relief (Anti-Anginal):
Beta-blockers are first-line agents; they relieve ischemia by reducing heart rate and myocardial contractility, which lowers oxygen demand and prolongs diastolic coronary perfusion time.
Calcium-Channel Blockers (CCBs) (either non-dihydropyridine or dihydropyridine) are excellent alternatives or add-ons that induce vasodilation.
Nitrates (short-acting sublingual or long-acting) relieve angina primarily by dilating capacitance veins, which decreases venous return (preload) and reduces ventricular wall stress.
Ranolazine can be used for refractory angina; it reduces intracellular calcium overload in myocytes, decreasing diastolic wall tension without affecting heart rate or blood pressure.
Revascularization (PCI vs. CABG): Interventions are indicated to improve symptoms when optimal medical therapy fails, or to improve survival in patients with high-risk anatomy.
Percutaneous Coronary Intervention (PCI) is favored for focal, less complex disease where rapid recovery is desired.
Coronary Artery Bypass Grafting (CABG) is preferred for left main coronary disease, complex multivessel disease (especially in diabetic patients where durable complete revascularization is critical), or when concomitant cardiac surgery (like valve replacement) is required.
Special Syndromes
Prinzmetal (Variant) Angina: Unlike typical angina, this is caused by transient vasospasm of the epicardial coronary arteries, often occurring at rest or at night, and producing transient ST-segment elevation on the ECG. It primarily affects young smokers lacking major atherosclerotic risk factors. Known triggers include cocaine, alcohol, and triptans. It is treated with CCBs (to prevent spasm) and nitrates; notably, beta-blockers are contraindicated because they leave alpha-adrenergic vasoconstriction unopposed and can worsen spasms, and aspirin is avoided as it can aggravate ischemic attacks.
Microvascular and Non-Obstructive Angina: Patients may present with classic angina and objective evidence of ischemia, but have absolutely no flow-limiting epicardial blockages on an angiogram (often termed INOCA or ANOCA). Microvascular angina results from impaired small-vessel dilation or abnormal microvascular constriction. Management requires careful risk-factor control and tailored antianginal therapy based on whether the dominant mechanism is impaired vasodilator reserve or hyper-constriction.

Pathophysiology of ACS
Plaque Vulnerability and Disruption Unlike Chronic Coronary Syndromes (CCS), which are typically driven by a stable, heavily fibrotic plaque that causes predictable exertional ischemia, Acute Coronary Syndromes (ACS) are triggered by the disruption of a vulnerable atherosclerotic plaque. A vulnerable plaque is characterized by a large lipid or necrotic core, intense macrophage-driven inflammation, decreased reparative smooth-muscle cells, and a dangerously thin fibrous cap. Because of macrophage-derived proteases degrading the matrix and mechanical stress concentrating at the plaque's shoulders, the plaque becomes highly susceptible to failure.
Thrombus Formation and Occlusion When the vulnerable plaque fails, it does so through either rupture (tearing of the fibrous cap, which exposes the highly thrombogenic necrotic core and tissue factor) or erosion (sloughing of the endothelial layer, exposing the subendothelial matrix). Both mechanisms expose circulating blood to thrombogenic material, instantly activating the coagulation cascade. Platelets adhere, activate, and release mediators like thromboxane A2 and adenosine diphosphate (ADP), recruiting more platelets. Simultaneously, thrombin converts fibrinogen into a fibrin mesh to stabilize the growing clot. Because coronary thrombosis is highly dynamic, the resulting thrombus can be fully occlusive, subtotal, or intermittent, and can embolize distally into the microcirculation.

Diagnostic Triage
Immediate 12-lead ECG The absolute first step in ACS triage is recognizing the symptoms and recording a 12-lead ECG as rapidly as possible. The ECG fundamentally dictates the emergency pathway by splitting presentations into those with persistent ST-segment elevation (requiring immediate reperfusion) and those without. If the initial tracing is non-diagnostic but symptoms continue, serial ECGs must be performed.
High-Sensitivity Cardiac Troponin Logic Cardiac troponins are highly sensitive and specific biomarkers for myocardial necrosis. However, the diagnostic logic for ACS relies heavily on observing a dynamic acute rise and/or fall in troponin levels, with at least one value exceeding the assay-specific 99th percentile, in the clinical context of ischemia.
A stable, chronic elevation suggests background structural heart disease or chronic kidney disease rather than an acute plaque event.
An acute elevation without signs of ischemia indicates acute myocardial injury from other causes, such as sepsis, myocarditis, or pulmonary embolism.
Crucial Rule: While troponin is required to differentiate UA from NSTEMI, reperfusion in a confirmed STEMI must never be delayed while waiting for a troponin result.

Clinical Classification
Unstable Angina (UA) UA represents acute myocardial ischemia without biomarker-defined necrosis (troponins remain negative). It presents as chest pain that is new in onset, accelerating in frequency or severity, or occurring at rest. The ECG shows no persistent ST elevation, though it may display transient ST depression or T-wave inversions. While less common in the era of high-sensitivity troponin assays, UA still signals significant plaque instability and impending infarction risk.
Non-ST-Segment Elevation Myocardial Infarction (NSTEMI) An NSTEMI occurs when a subtotal, intermittent, or microembolic occlusion causes actual myocardial necrosis, usually predominantly in the subendocardium. It is diagnosed when there is a positive rise/fall in cardiac biomarkers alongside ischemic symptoms or ECG changes (like ST depression or T-wave inversion), but without the persistent ST elevation seen in a STEMI.
ST-Segment Elevation Myocardial Infarction (STEMI) STEMI is the most critical ACS presentation, resulting from an acute, complete (or near-complete) occlusion of an epicardial coronary artery. This profound blockage leads to severe transmural ischemia and is diagnosed by persistent ST-segment elevation in anatomically contiguous ECG leads (or an equivalent occlusion pattern).

Treatment Pathways
Medical Stabilization (Antiplatelets, Anticoagulants, Anti-ischemics) All ACS patients require immediate medical stabilization to halt thrombus propagation and relieve ischemia:
Antiplatelets: Prompt loading of Aspirin is given unless contraindicated. This is combined with a P2Y12 inhibitor (such as ticagrelor, prasugrel, or clopidogrel) to provide dual antiplatelet therapy (DAPT). Ticagrelor and prasugrel are generally preferred over clopidogrel in suitable patients undergoing percutaneous coronary intervention (PCI).
Anticoagulants: Parenteral anticoagulation (e.g., unfractionated heparin, enoxaparin, bivalirudin, or fondaparinux) is administered during the acute phase to prevent fibrin propagation.
Anti-ischemics: Sublingual or intravenous nitrates are used for symptom relief (provided the patient is not hypotensive or suffering from a right ventricular infarct). Beta-blockers are initiated to reduce myocardial oxygen demand and mortality, provided the patient is hemodynamically stable and free of acute heart failure or cardiogenic shock.
Statins: High-intensity statin therapy is initiated early to stabilize the plaque and reduce long-term lipid-related risk.
Immediate Reperfusion for STEMI STEMI demands an emergency reperfusion strategy to limit infarct size and salvage myocardium:
Primary PCI is the preferred modality if it can be performed within a strict, guideline-defined timeframe by an experienced team (ideally door-to-balloon time <90 minutes). It mechanically restores flow and secures the vessel with a stent.
Fibrinolytic Therapy (e.g., alteplase or tenecteplase) is utilized for eligible patients presenting within the early time window (generally <12 hours from symptom onset) when timely PCI is completely unavailable. Because of bleeding risks (especially intracranial hemorrhage), strict contraindications must be respected.
Risk-Stratified Invasive Strategy for NSTE-ACS Because NSTE-ACS represents a wide spectrum of risk, angiography timing is tailored to the patient's clinical stability rather than a universal "immediate" rule:
Immediate Invasive Strategy (<2 hours): Required for very high-risk patients experiencing cardiogenic shock, hemodynamic instability, refractory/recurrent chest pain, life-threatening ventricular arrhythmias, or mechanical complications.
Early Invasive Strategy (<24 hours): Generally indicated for high-risk patients, such as those with confirmed troponin elevation (NSTEMI) or significant dynamic ST-segment changes.
Ischemia-Guided (Selective) Strategy: Lower-risk patients may be managed medically first, with angiography reserved only if they develop recurrent angina, hemodynamic instability, or positive objective non-invasive stress testing.

Myocardial Infarction Definition and Types Myocardial infarction (MI) is defined as irreversible cardiomyocyte death resulting from prolonged ischemia. While classically associated with plaque rupture, MI is classified into five distinct types based on the underlying pathophysiological mechanism:
Type 1: Acute atherothrombotic infarction caused by the disruption (rupture or erosion) of an atherosclerotic plaque, leading to superimposed coronary thrombosis.
Type 2: Infarction resulting from an oxygen supply-demand imbalance in the absence of acute atherothrombosis. Causes include severe anemia, hypoxemia, hypotension, sustained tachyarrhythmia, coronary vasospasm, or severe hypertension.
Type 3: Cardiac death accompanied by symptoms or electrocardiographic evidence highly suggestive of acute ischemia, occurring before cardiac biomarkers (troponin) can be measured or before they begin to rise.
Type 4: Infarction associated with percutaneous coronary intervention (PCI), which includes procedure-related infarction (e.g., side-branch occlusion) or acute stent thrombosis.
Type 5: Infarction associated with coronary artery bypass grafting (CABG) surgery.
Clinical Presentation and Atypical Features The classic presentation of an MI involves acute-onset, prolonged (>10–30 minutes) retrosternal pressure, heaviness, or tightness at rest. This discomfort frequently radiates to the left arm, shoulders, neck, jaw, back, or epigastrium. Associated autonomic and systemic symptoms are prominent, particularly diaphoresis (the most common associated symptom), breathlessness, nausea, vomiting, anxiety, and syncope.
However, atypical or "silent" (painless) presentations are incredibly common and dangerous. Patients may present solely with isolated dyspnea, unusual weakness, acute confusion/delirium, or isolated epigastric discomfort. These presentations are especially prevalent in:
Older adults, who may present primarily with decompensation of another organ system or altered mental status.
Patients with diabetes mellitus, because progressive autonomic neuropathy can blunt ischemic pain perception.
Women, who frequently report nausea, fatigue, or epigastric pain as their primary symptoms.
Heart transplant recipients, due to surgical denervation of the heart.
Electrocardiogram (ECG) Localization The ECG is the best initial diagnostic test. In a STEMI, the sequence of changes typically evolves from hyperacute (tall/peaked) T waves, to ST-segment elevation, followed by the development of pathologic Q waves, T-wave inversion, and eventually ST normalization. The anatomical location of the infarct is dictated by the specific leads showing ST-segment elevations or Q waves:
Anteroseptal (LAD occlusion): Leads V1–V2.
Anteroapical/Distal LAD: Leads V3–V4.
Anterolateral (LAD or LCx occlusion): Leads V5–V6.
Lateral (LCx occlusion): Leads I and aVL.
Inferior (RCA > LCx occlusion): Leads II, III, and aVF. Right-sided ECG leads (especially V4R) should be obtained to assess for concurrent right ventricular infarction, heavily associated with proximal RCA occlusions.
Posterior (PDA from RCA or LCx): Often manifests as reciprocal ST-segment depression in anterior leads (V1–V3) with dominant, tall R waves. Diagnosis is confirmed by obtaining posterior leads V7–V9 to visualize direct ST elevation.
Biomarker Kinetics Cardiac biomarkers are crucial for distinguishing MI from unstable angina, but they follow distinct temporal kinetics:
Cardiac Troponin (I and T): The most sensitive and specific markers for myocardial injury. They begin to rise 4 hours after symptom onset, peak between 24 to 48 hours, and remain elevated for 7 to 10 days. Because of this prolonged elevation, troponin is excellent for late diagnosis but poor for detecting early reinfarction.
Creatine Kinase-MB (CK-MB): Rises 6 to 12 hours after onset and peaks at 16 to 24 hours. Crucially, CK-MB levels return to normal within 48 hours. Therefore, CK-MB and the CK-MB/total CK ratio are the preferred markers for diagnosing a reinfarction occurring shortly after the initial event.
Histological Evolution The morphological changes of infarcted tissue evolve predictably, dictating the timeline of mechanical complications:
0–24 hours: Early coagulative necrosis begins between 4 and 24 hours, characterized by wavy fibers, cell content release, edema, and hemorrhage. If the tissue is reperfused, calcium influx and free radicals provoke hypercontraction of myofibrils, visible microscopically as dark, eosinophilic "contraction bands".
1–3 days: Extensive coagulative necrosis occurs. The tissue surrounding the infarct becomes acutely inflamed, marked by a dense infiltration of neutrophils.
3–14 days: Macrophages infiltrate the area to degrade and clear necrotic debris, followed by the formation of highly vascularized granulation tissue at the margins.
Weeks to months: The granulation tissue matures into a contracted, dense, gray-white collagenous scar.
Acute Stabilization and Reperfusion Immediate stabilization aims to halt thrombosis and reduce myocardial oxygen demand:
Antiplatelets and Anticoagulants: Prompt administration of Aspirin combined with a P2Y12 inhibitor (prasugrel or ticagrelor are preferred over clopidogrel if PCI is planned) is mandatory. Parenteral anticoagulation (e.g., heparin drip or LMWH) prevents further fibrin propagation.
Anti-ischemics: Sublingual or IV nitrates relieve angina and reduce preload, but they are strictly contraindicated in right ventricular (inferior) infarction due to severe preload dependence, as well as in hypotension or recent PDE-5 inhibitor use. Beta-blockers are administered to reduce mortality and demand, provided the patient is not in cardiogenic shock or acute heart failure.
Reperfusion Strategy: For STEMI, Primary PCI is the absolute preferred therapy, aiming for a door-to-balloon time of <90 minutes. If PCI cannot be performed within 120 minutes, Fibrinolytic therapy (e.g., alteplase, tenecteplase) is indicated if symptom onset is <12 hours, provided there are no absolute contraindications (such as previous intracranial hemorrhage, recent major surgery/trauma, or suspected aortic dissection).
Complication Timelines Post-MI complications are directly tied to the histological state of the myocardium:
First Day (0–24 hours): Life-threatening electrical instability. Ventricular fibrillation (VF) and ventricular tachycardia (VT) are the most common complications and the leading causes of sudden cardiac death before reaching the hospital. Cardiogenic shock and acute heart failure also peak here due to acute pump failure.
1–3 Days: As neutrophils infiltrate the epicardium, peri-infarction fibrinous pericarditis may develop, presenting with a friction rub and pleuritic chest pain. It is generally self-limited.
3–14 Days: This is the window of maximum mechanical vulnerability because macrophages structurally degrade the necrotic tissue before a firm scar can form. Complications include:
Papillary Muscle Rupture: Typically occurs 2–7 days post-MI. The posteromedial papillary muscle is overwhelmingly affected due to its single blood supply from the PDA (unlike the anterolateral muscle, which has dual supply). This causes acute, severe mitral regurgitation, flash pulmonary edema, and shock.
Ventricular Septal Rupture: Presents 3–5 days post-MI with a new harsh holosystolic murmur at the lower left sternal border, a left-to-right shunt, and an acute step-up in oxygen saturation in the right ventricle.
Left Ventricular Free-Wall Rupture: Occurs 5 days to 2 weeks post-MI, causing catastrophic hemopericardium, cardiac tamponade, pulseless electrical activity (PEA), and rapid death.
Weeks to Months: Dressler syndrome (an autoimmune fibrinous pericarditis driven by antibodies against released myocardial antigens) occurs 2 to 10 weeks post-MI. A true ventricular aneurysm may also form as the thinned fibrotic scar bulges outward during systole (dyskinesia), carrying a high risk of mural thrombus formation, persistent ST-segment elevation, and late arrhythmias.

Antithrombotic Therapy
Single Antiplatelet for Stable Disease: For patients with established chronic coronary disease who do not have a recent acute event or intervention, long-term single antiplatelet therapy (most commonly aspirin) is the standard of care to prevent atherothrombotic events, provided the patient does not have a high bleeding risk.
Dual Antiplatelet Therapy (DAPT) Duration and Selection: DAPT, which combines aspirin with a P2Y12 receptor inhibitor, is indicated after an acute coronary syndrome (ACS) or percutaneous coronary intervention (PCI) to prevent stent thrombosis and spontaneous recurrent atherothrombotic events. Among the P2Y12 inhibitors, ticagrelor and prasugrel offer more potent and predictable platelet inhibition and are generally preferred over clopidogrel for suitable ACS patients undergoing PCI. The default duration for DAPT is typically 12 months for patients with a low bleeding risk. However, the exact duration must be highly individualized: it may be shortened or de-escalated for patients facing high bleeding risks, or extended beyond 12 months for those who have a high ischemic risk and an acceptably low bleeding risk.
Lipid Management
High-Intensity Statins: High-intensity or maximally tolerated statin therapy is a foundational component of secondary prevention. Statins inhibit hepatic cholesterol synthesis, which not only lowers circulating low-density lipoprotein (LDL) cholesterol but also decreases vascular inflammation, improves endothelial function, and promotes plaque stabilization, rendering plaque rupture less likely.
Thresholds for Non-Statin Additions: Following an ACS, a fasting lipid panel should be repeated 4 to 8 weeks after therapy initiation or adjustment to verify the biological response. If LDL cholesterol remains at or above 70 mg/dL despite maximally tolerated statin therapy (or at even lower thresholds for select very-high-risk patients), guidelines recommend the addition of non-statin therapies. Options include ezetimibe, which decreases intestinal cholesterol absorption, and PCSK9 inhibitors, which preserve hepatic LDL receptors to produce profound additional LDL reductions.
Neurohormonal Blockade and LV Protection
ACE Inhibitors / ARBs: These agents inhibit the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system, thereby reducing vasoconstriction, sodium retention, and maladaptive ventricular remodeling. They are strongly indicated for secondary prevention in patients whose coronary disease coexists with a reduced left ventricular (LV) ejection fraction, an anterior myocardial infarction, hypertension, diabetes mellitus, or chronic kidney disease.
Beta-Blockers: Beta-blockers reduce myocardial oxygen demand by lowering heart rate, contractility, and blood pressure, making them highly effective antianginal agents. However, routine long-term beta-blocker therapy is not automatically mandated solely to improve prognosis in every patient with chronic coronary disease. Their use for prognostic benefit and ventricular protection is most firmly established for patients who have had a recent myocardial infarction, have a reduced LV ejection fraction, or have concurrent indications such as hypertension or arrhythmias.
Device Therapy Timing
Implantable Cardioverter-Defibrillator (ICD) Criteria: An ICD continuously monitors the ventricular rhythm to detect and terminate life-threatening arrhythmias, preventing sudden cardiac death. For primary prevention, decisions regarding ICD placement must be deferred until after an appropriate period of recovery and optimization of medical therapy, because stunned myocardium may spontaneously regain function early after an infarction or revascularization. Once reassessed, an ICD is typically indicated for symptomatic patients (NYHA class II or III) with an EF < 35%, or asymptomatic patients (NYHA class I) with an EF < 30%, provided they are at least 40 days post-MI and 3 months post-revascularization. For secondary prevention, an ICD is indicated for survivors of cardiac arrest or sustained hemodynamically important ventricular tachycardia when the event was not caused solely by a reversible trigger.
Lifestyle Modifications
Smoking Cessation: Halting tobacco exposure is critical because smoking promotes endothelial dysfunction, oxidative stress, inflammation, vasoconstriction, and thrombosis, while also reducing the blood's oxygen-carrying capacity. Behavioral support and evidence-based pharmacotherapy are recommended; however, electronic cigarettes are not preferred as first-line cessation aids due to uncertain long-term cardiovascular and pulmonary safety.
Cardiac Rehabilitation: Structured cardiac rehabilitation programs are strongly recommended after an ACS or revascularization whenever feasible. These programs provide supervised exercise prescriptions alongside risk-factor management, nutritional intervention, medication support, psychological care, and return-to-work planning. Participation improves functional capacity, endothelial function, autonomic balance, and quality of life while reducing recurrent cardiovascular morbidity.
